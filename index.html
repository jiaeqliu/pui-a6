Surviving Stroke

A Visual Guide for Patients and Caretakers

Every 40 seconds, someone in the United States has a stroke. The third leading cause of death in the US, more than 140,000 people die each year from it.

Despite its enormous impact on public health, the data regarding stroke can be difficult for patients, family members, and caretakers to understand. Sometimes, statistics are quoted without critical context. Other times, the data is highly technical and buried deep within medical journal articles.

This guide was created to help readers visualize the available data and understand the causes and long-term outlook for stroke patients.

Stroke is a leading cause of death in the US.

A stroke occurs when blood flow to an area of the brain is cut off, causing brain cells to die from a lack of oxygen. Where a stroke occurs, and how much the brain is damaged, determines how the person is affected.

Stroke is the 4th leading cause of death ages 65 and older. However, among the oldest adults (85+), heart disease and cancer are far more common causes of death.

Stroke deaths have been decreasing, but they are also distributed unevenly by race and gender.

Stroke risk is influenced by a variety of behavioral factors, such as obesity, smoking, and exercise, which are strongly correlated with socioeconomic and demographic factors.

Ischemic stroke is by far the most common type of stroke.

Ischemic strokes are when blood clots form and block a blood vessel. Hemorrhagic strokes are rarer and occur when blood vessels rupture. Although hemorrhagic strokes are rarer, they more often lead to death.

The outcomes for ischemic strokes vary by cause.

Because ischemic strokes are far more common, more definitive research has been conducted to link the underlying cause of the stroke to long-term outcomes. The following data is drawn from the German Stroke Data Bank, which includes a comprehensive patient data set for ischemic stroke.

Patient outcomes are scored using the Modified Rankin Scale (mRS), which measures the degree of disability or dependence in the daily activities of people who have suffered a stroke or other causes of neurological disability. The scale ranges from 0 (No symptoms) to 6 (Deceased).

Unfortunately, the data for hemorrhagic stroke is more limited.

There is ongoing research into mapping the causes of these strokes to long-term outcomes. However, due to their relative rarity, and higher mortality rates, the data is morel imited.
